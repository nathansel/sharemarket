Chapter 01 · Foundation

What is a Poor Man's
Covered Call?

Imagine you want to rent out a house you don't yet own. You sign a contract to buy the house later at today's price, then immediately start charging rent. That's essentially what a PMCC does — but with Nifty options.

The Chai Tapri Explanation

Let's forget financial jargon for a moment. Imagine three people at a tea stall discussing Nifty:

Person A — The Big Investor (Traditional Covered Call)

Buys 75 units of Nifty at ₹24,000 each = ₹18,00,000. Then charges a monthly "rent" by selling a call option for ₹60 per unit = ₹4,500 per month. But who has ₹18 lakhs sitting idle?

Person B — The Smart Trader (PMCC)

Instead of buying all 75 units at full price, buys the RIGHT to own them later at ₹22,000 strike — pays only ₹2,400 per unit = ₹1,80,000 total. Then charges the same "rent" by selling the same call option. Same income. Only 10% of the capital.

Person C — The Option Buyer (Just buying a call)

Just buys the right to profit from Nifty rising. Pays premium, prays Nifty moves up fast, gets no income along the way. Watches money erode every day due to time decay.

🏆
The PMCC Sweet Spot
Person B gets the best of both worlds — leveraged exposure like a futures trader, regular income like a covered call writer, but with strictly defined, limited maximum loss. This is why the PMCC is called the strategy for the "poor man" — it doesn't require crores of rupees.

Why Traditional Covered Calls Don't Work in India

In the US, you can buy 100 shares of Apple and sell a call against them. Simple. But in India, for Nifty:

The 30-Second Summary

1
Buy a "cheap version" of holding Nifty
Purchase a deep In-The-Money (ITM) call option expiring 2–3 months from now. This option tracks Nifty's price movement almost like actually holding it, but costs a fraction of the price.
2
Collect rent by selling a short-term call
Every week (or month), sell an Out-of-The-Money (OTM) call option with a higher strike price. You receive a premium instantly. This is your "rental income."
3
Short call expires → collect, repeat
If Nifty doesn't go above your short call's strike by expiry, you keep the full premium. Roll the short call to the next expiry and collect again.
4
Manage & adjust as market moves
If Nifty rallies hard, roll your short call up. If it crashes, your max loss is already defined — no margin calls, no panic. Adjust patiently and continue.

Chapter 02 · Foundation

Options 101 —
Everything a Beginner Needs

You cannot trade PMCC without understanding options basics. This chapter explains every key concept using simple analogies from everyday Indian life — no jargon, no confusion.

What is an Option?

An option is a contract that gives you a right, but not an obligation, to buy or sell something at a specific price on or before a specific date — in exchange for paying a fee called a premium.

🏠
Real-life analogy: Flat booking advance
You're interested in buying a flat at ₹50 lakh. You pay ₹1 lakh as "advance" (booking amount). This gives you the RIGHT to buy the flat at ₹50 lakh for the next 3 months. If prices rise to ₹60 lakh — you exercise your right and profit. If prices fall — you walk away and lose only the ₹1 lakh advance. That advance is the option premium.

Two Types of Options

📈 Call Option (CE)
→Right to BUY the underlying at strike price
→Profits when market goes up
→Buyer pays premium, seller receives it
→Example: Nifty 25,000 CE = right to "buy" Nifty at 25,000
★PMCC uses both a long and short call
📉 Put Option (PE)
→Right to SELL the underlying at strike price
→Profits when market goes down
→Used for hedging or bearish views
→Example: Nifty 23,000 PE = protection below 23,000
✗Not directly used in PMCC (but useful for hedging)

Key Terms You Must Know

Strike Price
The price at which you have the right to buy/sell. Example: "Nifty 24,500 CE" — strike = 24,500. Nifty must go above 24,500 for this call to have value at expiry.
Premium
The price you pay to buy an option, or receive when you sell one. Quoted per unit. For Nifty, multiply by 75 (lot size) for total money. Example: ₹60 premium × 75 = ₹4,500 total.
Expiry / DTE
The date the option contract ends. Nifty weekly options expire every Tuesday. DTE = Days To Expiry. A "7 DTE" option expires in 7 days.
ITM (In The Money)
For a call: when Nifty is ABOVE the strike price. Example: Nifty = 24,000, Strike = 22,000 → This call is ITM by 2,000 points. Has intrinsic value.
ATM (At The Money)
When Nifty is at (or very close to) the strike price. Highest time value, most sensitive to price movement.
OTM (Out of The Money)
For a call: when Nifty is BELOW the strike price. Example: Nifty = 24,000, Strike = 25,000 → OTM by 1,000. Has only time value, no intrinsic value.
Lot Size
One Nifty options contract = 75 units. All P&L calculations multiply by 75. If premium is ₹100, you pay ₹100 × 75 = ₹7,500 total.
Intrinsic Value
The "real" value of an option = how much ITM it is. Nifty at 24,000, Strike 22,000 CE = ₹2,000 intrinsic value. This never goes negative.
Time Value (Extrinsic)
The "hope" portion of premium. Even an ITM option has some time value. This decays to zero at expiry. Near-dated options decay faster — crucial for PMCC.
Diagonal Spread
A strategy using two options with DIFFERENT expiry dates AND different strikes. PMCC is a diagonal spread — long-dated ITM call + short-dated OTM call.

Understanding Premium: Where Does the Money Come From?

An option's premium has two components. Understanding this is critical for PMCC:

Option Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value (Extrinsic Value) Example: Nifty = 24,000 | 22,000 CE premium = ₹2,450 Intrinsic Value: 24,000 – 22,000 = ₹2,000 Time Value: ₹2,450 – ₹2,000 = ₹450 → Deep ITM calls have high intrinsic, low time value (good for long leg) → OTM calls have zero intrinsic, all time value (good for short leg — it all decays)

Chapter 03 · Foundation

The PMCC Structure —
How It's Built

Understanding the exact mechanics of both legs, why the initialization rule is sacred, and how profit and loss are calculated. This is the engineering behind the strategy.

The Two Legs Explained

Leg 1: Long Call (The Foundation)

What: Buy a deep ITM call option with far expiry (60–120+ days out)

Why deep ITM: High delta (0.75–0.90) means it moves almost like Nifty itself — this is your "synthetic Nifty position"

Why far expiry: Slow time decay. You're paying for time — you want it to last long enough to sell multiple short calls against it

Example: Nifty at 24,000 → Buy 22,000 CE expiring in 3 months @ ₹2,400 premium

Leg 2: Short Call (The Income Engine)

What: Sell an OTM call option with near expiry (7–21 days)

Why OTM: Lower probability of Nifty breaching this strike, so you keep the premium most of the time

Why near expiry: Fast time decay works IN YOUR FAVOR — you receive premium that decays quickly, so you can sell again soon

Example: Nifty at 24,000 → Sell 25,000 CE expiring next Tuesday @ ₹60 premium

The Sacred Initialization Rule

⚖️ The Rule That Must Never Be Broken
Strike Width = Short call strike – Long call strike Net Debit = Long call premium – Short call premium received RULE: Strike Width MUST be GREATER than Net Debit paid If Strike Width > Net Debit → Trade is VALID ✓ (max profit achievable) If Strike Width < Net Debit → Trade is BROKEN ✗ (cannot profit even at best case) Example: Strike Width = 25,000 – 22,000 = 3,000 Net Debit = 2,400 – 60 = 2,340 3,000 > 2,340 → VALID TRADE ✓
⛔
Why this rule matters
If Strike Width < Net Debit, then even if Nifty goes to the moon and both legs are at max value, closing the position gives you less than what you paid. You've built a structurally losing trade. Check this BEFORE every single PMCC entry — no exceptions.

Complete P&L Formulas

Net Debit (Capital at Risk) = Long premium – Short premium Maximum Profit = (Strike Width – Net Debit) × Lot Size Maximum Loss = Net Debit × Lot Size Breakeven at Expiry = Long call strike + Net Debit Scenario: Nifty 24,000 | Long 22,000CE @2400 | Short 25,000CE @60 Net Debit: 2,400 – 60 = ₹2,340/unit Max Profit: (3,000–2,340) × 75 = ₹49,500 (Nifty must be at/above 25,000) Max Loss: 2,340 × 75 = ₹1,75,500 (Nifty crashes to zero) Breakeven: 22,000 + 2,340 = 24,340 (Nifty must be above this at long expiry)

Each time the short call expires worthless, you keep the premium. This reduces your effective cost basis — making you more profitable over time even if Nifty stays flat.

Step-by-Step Entry Process

1
Check India VIX first (go to NSE website or Kite)
VIX between 13–22: Proceed. VIX below 13: premiums too cheap, skip. VIX above 25: too risky, wait. India VIX is your weather forecast for options trading.
2
Check Nifty trend on daily chart
Is Nifty above its 20-day EMA? Is it in an uptrend or sideways? PMCC works best when Nifty is neutral to mildly bullish. Strong downtrend = skip for now.
3
Select the Long Call (Far month, Deep ITM)
Open the Nifty option chain for 2–3 months out. Find the strike that is 5–8% below current Nifty. Check its delta — should be 0.75–0.90. Note the ask price (you'll buy at this).
4
Select the Short Call (Near week, OTM)
Open the Nifty option chain for this week or next week's expiry. Find the strike 2–4% above current Nifty. Check delta — should be 0.20–0.30. Note the bid price (you'll sell at this).
5
Verify the Initialization Rule
Calculate: Strike Width (short – long) must exceed Net Debit (long premium – short premium). If valid, proceed. If not, adjust strikes.
6
Place orders — Long call first, then Short call
Use LIMIT orders only — never market orders on options (bid-ask spread is wide). Leg in the long call first (buy). Then immediately sell the short call. Ideally, both executed within 2–3 minutes of each other.
7
Set alerts and management schedule
Set Kite/Sensibull alerts: (a) Nifty approaches short call strike − 200 pts, (b) Short call reaches 50% profit, (c) Long call DTE = 45 days. Review position every Tuesday (expiry day) and Friday.

Greeks Simplified —
No Math Required

Option Greeks sound intimidating but each one answers a simple question. Master these four and you'll know exactly what your PMCC position will do under any market condition.

Delta — The Movement Meter

Simple Analogy: Your car's speedometer

Delta tells you: For every ₹1 Nifty moves, how much does your option move?

Delta = 0.85 → Nifty moves ₹100 → Your option moves ₹85

Delta = 0.25 → Nifty moves ₹100 → Your option moves ₹25

PositionDeltaWhat it means
Long 22,000 CE (deep ITM)+0.85Gains ₹85 per unit for every ₹100 Nifty rise
Short 25,000 CE (OTM)-0.25Loses ₹25 per unit for every ₹100 Nifty rise (cost of short)
Net PMCC position+0.60Net gains ₹60 per unit for every ₹100 Nifty rise
🎯
Pro Tip: Delta as Probability
Delta also approximates the probability of an option expiring ITM. Short call delta = 0.25 means ~25% chance it expires ITM (bad for you). So you're right 75% of the time when you sell delta-0.25 calls. This is why 0.20–0.30 delta is the sweet spot.

Theta — The Time Decay Clock

Simple Analogy: Ice melting in a glass

Theta tells you: How much premium does an option lose PER DAY due to time passing?

Far-dated options melt slowly. Near-dated options melt fast. Theta is ALWAYS negative for option buyers (you lose value each day). Theta is POSITIVE for option sellers (you gain from decay).

Vega — The Volatility Sensor

Simple Analogy: Blood pressure monitor

Vega tells you: How much does your option change in value for every 1% change in India VIX?

Rising VIX = options get more expensive. Your long call (far-dated) has HIGH vega — it benefits MORE from rising VIX than your short call loses. Net PMCC has positive vega = benefits from rising VIX.

⚡
Key Insight
Enter PMCC when VIX is LOW (14–16) — your long call is cheaper. Sell short calls when VIX is HIGH (18–22) — collect richer premiums. Buy low-vol long, sell high-vol short = maximum edge.

Gamma — The Delta Accelerator

Simple Analogy: Car's acceleration pedal

Gamma tells you: How fast does delta change as Nifty moves?

Near-expiry ATM options have HIGH gamma — delta changes rapidly. Far-dated options have LOW gamma — delta changes slowly. For PMCC:

  • Your LONG leg has low gamma (good — stable delta, predictable)
  • Your SHORT leg has high gamma near expiry (risk — it can move against you quickly)
  • This is why you roll the short call BEFORE expiry — to avoid high gamma risk

The Adjustments Playbook —
When and How to Act

Knowing when to adjust separates the 10% of PMCC traders who profit consistently from the 90% who don't. Every trigger has a specific, pre-planned response. Emotion has no place here.

The Golden Rule of Adjustment

🥇 Always Have a Plan BEFORE You Need One

Every PMCC position you enter should come with pre-written answers to: "What do I do if Nifty goes up 3%? Down 5%? Stays flat?" Decide these answers when you're calm. Don't make them when the market is moving fast and your P&L is blinking red.

Short Call Adjustments

Adjustment 1: Roll Out (Most Common — ~3x/month)

Trigger: Short call reaches 50% profit OR only 5 DTE remaining (whichever comes first)

StepActionDetails
1Buy back current short callPlace limit order at current ask. Pay to close.
2Sell next week's OTM callSame strike (25,000 CE) or 1 strike higher if Nifty moved up. Collect new premium.
3Verify net credit or small debitThe roll should ideally be for net credit (you collect more than you pay). Small debit is OK.

Why 50% profit rule? After capturing 50% of max short call profit, the remaining 50% represents much higher risk (gamma spikes near expiry). Better to close, take 50%, and sell a fresh weekly option. Over 12 cycles, 50% × 12 = 6x premium vs waiting for 100% × 8 cycles = 8x... but with far lower stress and risk.

Trigger: Nifty approaches within 200 points of short call strike

StepActionCost/Benefit

The Roll-Up Rule
Only roll up if the new short call strike is still BELOW the long call's strike AND the net position delta is still positive. Never roll into a position where short call strike exceeds long call strike — that creates an inverted risk profile.

Adjustment 3: Emergency Roll-Up (Short Call Deep ITM)

Trigger: Nifty surges 5%+ in one move. Short call is 500+ points ITM.

Context: This feels scary but the P&L is likely near max profit because the long call gained much more than the short call lost.

1
Check net P&L first
Your long call at delta 0.85 gained ~0.85 × 1,200 = ₹1,020/unit. Short call lost ~(1,200−60) = ₹1,140/unit. Net = small loss on this roll, but position is near max profit overall. Do the math before panic-closing.
2
Decision: Close everything or roll up aggressively
If P&L > 40% of max profit: Close both legs. Lock the gain. If P&L = 20–40%: Roll short call to 26,000–26,500 CE in next month. Collect new premium at higher strike.
3
Accept the roll cost
Rolling up 2 strikes in next expiry will have a cost (net debit on the roll). This is acceptable because your long call has appreciated significantly to offset it.

Long Call Adjustments

Adjustment 4: Roll Forward the Long Call (Critical — Do this every 3 months)

Trigger: Long call reaches 30–45 DTE remaining

Why it's critical: In the last 30 days, theta (time decay) on the long call ACCELERATES sharply. You're now paying for time much faster. The PMCC's structural edge (short call decays faster than long call) breaks down in this period.

How to Roll Forward: Step 1: Close existing long call (Sell 22,000 CE, 30 DTE remaining) Receive: ~₹1,800/unit (time value mostly gone, but intrinsic value captured) Step 2: Buy new long call (Buy 22,000 CE, 90 DTE from now) Pay: ~₹2,600/unit (fresh time value at current Nifty level) Step 3: Net cost of roll: ₹2,600 – ₹1,800 = ₹800/unit additional capital required → Worth it because you've extended the PMCC lifecycle by 60 more days of income

Trigger: Nifty has crashed 8%+ and long call lost 40%+ value. You still believe in recovery.

Warning: Only do this if you have strong fundamental conviction that Nifty will recover. Rolling down is increasing exposure on a losing trade — ensure this isn't just hope.

1. I have only ₹1.5 lakh. Can I trade Nifty PMCC?
▼
Yes, ₹1.5 lakh is enough for one Nifty PMCC lot in some strike configurations. The net debit for a Nifty PMCC (e.g., Buy 22,000 CE @ ₹2,000, Sell 25,000 CE @ ₹60) = ₹1,940 × 75 = ₹1,45,500. You need at least ₹1.5–2 lakh to be safe. However, with only ₹1.5 lakh, you have very little buffer for adjustments. Recommendation: Start with paper trading first. Build to ₹3 lakh before live trading PMCC for real. With ₹3 lakh, you can run a PMCC AND have enough capital for rolling adjustments without panic.
2. What happens if Nifty crashes 20% and my long call goes worthless?
▼
This is the max loss scenario. If Nifty crashes from 24,000 to 19,200 (−20%), your 22,000 CE (3-month expiry) becomes deeply OTM and may be worth only ₹50–100 (pure time value). But your maximum loss is ALREADY CAPPED at the net debit you paid. You cannot lose more than ₹1,75,500 on a standard Nifty PMCC. Compare this to futures: a 20% crash on Nifty futures = −₹3,60,000 loss with margin calls. PMCC's defined risk is its greatest feature. This is why we always say: PMCC is not "if I lose" but "how much I can lose." The answer is always fixed and known from day one.
3. Can I be "assigned" on my short Nifty call? I'm scared of assignment.
▼
NO! This is the most important India-specific advantage. Nifty 50 options are EUROPEAN style and CASH SETTLED. This means: (1) They can only be exercised at expiry — not before. (2) Settlement is in cash, not in actual Nifty shares. So even if your 25,000 CE short call is 1,000 points ITM, nobody can "call it away" early. At expiry, the cash difference is settled automatically by NSE. You will never receive an assignment notice or be forced to deliver anything before expiry. This eliminates the biggest risk in US-style PMCC trading entirely.
4. What is "rolling" and how do I do it on Zerodha?
▼
Rolling means closing your current short call and opening a new short call for a different strike or expiry. On Zerodha Kite: (1) Open the F&O market watchlist. (2) For the buyback: Select your existing short call (25,000 CE, current week). Click "Buy" to close the short position. Use limit order at or near the ask price. (3) For the new sell: Select next week's 25,000 CE (or 25,500 CE). Click "Sell" to open the new short position. Use limit order at or near the bid price. (4) Execute both within 2–3 minutes of each other. Alternatively, Sensibull.com has a visual "roll" feature that shows the net credit/debit of rolling before you place the order.
5. Should I sell weekly or monthly short calls?
▼
For beginners: Start with monthly short calls (4-week expiry). Less frequent management, more time to react. For intermediate traders: Weekly short calls (7 DTE) generate more premium over time because theta decay is faster in the final week. The math: Selling 4 weekly options at ₹60 each = ₹240/month vs one monthly at ₹150. Weekly wins over time — but requires more monitoring. Professional recommendation: Use 7–14 DTE short calls (weekly to bi-weekly). Roll at 50% profit or 5 DTE, whichever comes first. This is the optimal sweet spot between income and management effort.
6. How do I choose the right strike for the short call?
▼
The golden rule: Short call delta between 0.20 and 0.30. This means: (1) ~70–80% probability the short call expires worthless (you win). (2) Enough premium to be worth selling (typically ₹40–80 per unit on Nifty). (3) Enough buffer above current Nifty for normal weekly moves. Practically: If Nifty is at 24,000, find the strike where the CE is priced at ₹40–80 in the weekly option chain. That's typically 1.5–3% above spot for Nifty. You can also directly look at the delta column in Sensibull's option chain — find the strike closest to 0.25 delta.
7. What if Nifty gaps up 4% overnight due to global news?
▼
This is actually a favorable scenario for PMCC! Here's why: Your long call (deep ITM, delta 0.85) captures 85% of the gap-up move. Your short call (OTM) only loses partially (delta 0.25 of the gap). Net: You gain more on the long than you lose on the short. On gap-up open: (1) Don't panic. (2) Check your net P&L — it's likely positive. (3) If short call is now ITM: buy it back and sell a new OTM short call at 26,000 CE or similar. (4) Let the long call appreciation do the heavy lifting. European settlement means you have all day to plan and no assignment pressure.
8. Can I run PMCC on Bank Nifty instead of Nifty?
▼
Yes, but with caveats. Bank Nifty is more volatile than Nifty (moves 400–800 pts daily vs 100–300 for Nifty). This means: PROS: Richer premiums on short calls (more income potential). CONS: Long call loses value faster on corrections; requires closer monitoring; Bank Nifty no longer has weekly expiry (only monthly as of 2025). For beginners: Start with Nifty. Nifty is more predictable and has weekly expiry options which give more premium cycles per quarter. For advanced traders: Running both Nifty PMCC and BankNifty PMCC simultaneously is a common professional approach for income diversification.
9. How do taxes work for PMCC income in India?
▼
PMCC income in India is classified as Speculative Business Income (for intraday-like profits/losses) or Non-speculative Business Income (for F&O trades held overnight). Key points: (1) F&O gains/losses are treated as BUSINESS INCOME — not capital gains. (2) They are taxed at your applicable income tax slab rate (20%, 30%, etc.). (3) You can offset F&O losses against other business income (not salary). (4) You must file ITR-3 if you have F&O activity. (5) Premium received on short calls is income when realized. (6) Consult a CA who specializes in F&O taxation — this is a complex area. (7) Use Zerodha Tradebook or Quicko.com for automatic F&O P&L statements for tax filing.
10. What is the best time of day to place PMCC orders?
▼
Optimal windows: 10:00 AM – 11:30 AM: Best liquidity, market has stabilized from open, bid-ask spreads tightest. 2:00 PM – 3:00 PM: Second-best window, institutional activity picks up. Avoid: 9:15–9:30 AM (wild swings, wide spreads, irrational prices), 3:00–3:30 PM on expiry day (final settlement volatility). For rolling short calls: Tuesday 10 AM–12 PM is ideal (expiry day, liquidity peaks, you can roll the expiring short call and open next week's in the same session).
11. How much time per week does PMCC management require?
▼
Realistic time commitment: Entry week: 2–3 hours (research, setup, order placement). Weekly management (no adjustment needed): 30–45 minutes (check levels, check alerts, note P&L). Weekly management (adjustment needed): 1–2 hours (analyze, plan roll, execute). Long call roll (every 3 months): 1–2 hours. Total per week on average: 1–2 hours. This is far less than active intraday trading. PMCC is designed for people with jobs or other commitments who want a systematic income strategy without screen-watching all day.
12. What is India VIX and how exactly should I use it for PMCC?
▼
India VIX (Volatility Index) measures the market's expectation of Nifty volatility over the next 30 days. It's calculated from Nifty option prices — when options are expensive (high demand), VIX rises. For PMCC: VIX 13–16: Low volatility. Short call premiums are cheap. Still tradeable but income is lower. Consider slightly closer strikes (delta 0.30–0.35) for more premium. VIX 16–20: Sweet spot. Good balance of premium income and manageable risk. VIX 20–25: Elevated. Rich premiums! Sell OTM calls at wider strikes (delta 0.20–0.25). More income, lower breach risk. VIX >25: Danger zone. Premiums look great but this signals a real crisis. Long call may crash in value faster than short call income compensates. Best to stay out or use smaller lot size. You can track India VIX live at nseindia.com or directly on Zerodha Kite.
13. Can I run multiple PMCC positions simultaneously?
▼
Yes, professional PMCC traders often run 2–4 positions simultaneously. Common approaches: (1) Same index, staggered expiries: Enter one Nifty PMCC in January with March long call, another in February with April long call. Staggering gives you income every week regardless of market conditions. (2) Different indices: Run Nifty PMCC + BankNifty PMCC for diversification. (3) Individual stocks + index: Run Nifty PMCC + PMCC on a high-delta stock like Reliance. Guidelines: Never put more than 30–40% total capital across all PMCC positions. Each position max 15% of capital. With ₹5L account: max 2 Nifty lots or 1 Nifty + 1 BankNifty. With ₹10L account: 3–4 positions is manageable.
14. What happens if I forget to roll my short call before expiry?
▼
If you forget to roll the short call and it expires: (a) If it expired worthless (OTM): Great! You keep the premium. No action needed. Just sell next week's short call as planned. (b) If it expired ITM: NSE automatically settles it in cash on expiry. The loss (intrinsic value of ITM call) is automatically debited from your account. Your long call is still open. You must now sell a new short call (at appropriate OTM strike) for the next cycle. No delivery or shares involved — just cash settlement. The lesson: Set Tuesday calendar alerts so you never forget. Missing a roll is not catastrophic but losing money on an avoidable ITM short call settlement stings.
15. Is PMCC better than just selling a naked call for income?
▼
Absolutely — and here's why. Naked call selling on Nifty: Income: ₹4,500/week (same as PMCC short call). Risk: UNLIMITED. If Nifty rises 500 pts past your strike, you lose ₹37,500+. If Nifty rises 2,000 pts (bear market rally or black swan), you lose ₹1,50,000+ with no cap. Margin required: ₹1,20,000–1,50,000 (SEBI SPAN margins). PMCC with long call: Income: Same ₹4,500/week. Risk: Capped at net debit (₹1,75,500 max). If Nifty rises 2,000 pts, your long call gains offset the short call loss — you're near MAX PROFIT. Capital: ₹1,75,500 (slightly more, but defined forever). The long call acts as an insurance policy for your short call. ALWAYS prefer PMCC over naked calls.

50 Expert Tips for Nifty PMCC Traders

Strategy & Entry

Tip 1
Enter PMCC when India VIX is rising from lows (13–15 moving to 17–18). You buy the long call at low IV and immediately sell the first short call at higher, rising IV. Double edge.
Tip 2
The long call should be so deep ITM that when Nifty moves ₹100, your long call moves at least ₹75. If it moves less than ₹65 per ₹100 Nifty move, go deeper ITM.
Tip 3
Never enter a PMCC within 5 days of the long call's own expiry. Always ensure long leg has 45+ DTE at the time of ANY short call sale.
Tip 4
After the Union Budget announcement, IV typically drops sharply (IV crush). Wait 2–3 days post-budget before entering new PMCC — premiums stabilize to rational levels.
Tip 5
The best PMCC entries on Nifty historically come after a 5–8% correction from recent highs — when Nifty is oversold but fundamentals are intact. Your long call costs less and breakeven is lower.

Management & Rolling

Tip 6
The 20% rule: If you can buy back the short call for 20% of what you sold it for, close it. If you sold at ₹60 and it's now ₹12, buy it back and sell next week's fresh. The remaining ₹12 risk isn't worth holding another 5 days for ₹12 gain when you can start a new ₹60 cycle.
Tip 7
Never roll your short call on the same day you plan to roll your long call. Execute long leg roll on Monday. Execute short leg roll on Tuesday (expiry). Separating these prevents compounding confusion.
Tip 8
Keep a rolling journal: date, Nifty level, short call sold at, strike, premium, buyback date, buyback cost, net premium. After 10 cycles you'll see clear patterns in which market conditions generate the best income.
Tip 9
The Diwali season (Oct–Nov) is historically the strongest period for Nifty PMCC. Market tends to rally, VIX is moderate, premiums are consistent. Plan your largest PMCC allocation for this window.
Tip 10
When rolling up your short call after a rally, always sell the new short call in the NEXT expiry, not the same expiry. This gives you more time value and avoids the risk of the short call expiring ITM in the current week.
